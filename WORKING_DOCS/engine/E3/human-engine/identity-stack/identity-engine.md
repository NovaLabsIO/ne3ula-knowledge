# Identity Engine

Identity Engine defines how identity functions dynamically inside the E3 human
engine.

If Identity Core provides the authored center, Identity Engine explains how that
center is translated into lived rhythm, challenged by reality, and integrated
over time.

## Purpose

The Identity Engine exists to interpret movement between:

- chosen identity
- actual behavior
- recurring friction
- stabilizing rituals
- longer-term pattern change

It treats identity as something that must be practiced, not merely declared.

## Core Dynamic Functions

The archive consistently points to five recurring functions:

### Friction Mapping

Friction reveals where lived behavior and authored identity are not fully in
sync.

Not all friction is misalignment. Some friction indicates growth pressure.
The engine therefore reads friction as diagnostic signal rather than as failure.

### Mode Recognition

Modes describe the participant's current operational emphasis, especially around
people, pleasure, and production.

Modes are situational states, not identity primitives.
They help explain how identity is currently being expressed or constrained.

### Ritual Design

Rituals stabilize identity in practice.

They translate chosen principles into repeatable actions, making coherence more
durable than intention alone.

### Drift Detection

Drift appears when choices, language, or rhythm start expressing a less-authored
pattern.

The engine treats drift as a call for reconnection, not a moral defect.

### Integration Prompts

Prompts help the participant interpret what a pattern means and what return may
require.

Their role is reflective, not deterministic.

## Identity Movement

Identity shifts through a cycle:

1. A participant authors or revises a core orientation.
2. Daily life exposes the gap between intention and embodiment.
3. Friction, mode, and drift signals reveal where the pattern is stable,
   strained, or fragmented.
4. Ritual and reflection support return or revision.
5. Over time, repeated practice either integrates the identity more deeply or
   reveals that the core itself needs refinement.

## Stabilization and Fragmentation

Identity tends to stabilize when:

- behavior repeatedly matches authored commitments
- rituals support consistency across contexts
- the participant can tolerate friction without abandoning authorship
- reflection leads to revision rather than avoidance

Identity tends to fragment when:

- external scripts override chosen values
- modes are mistaken for the self
- repeated drift goes unrecognized
- friction is interpreted only as shame, not information

## Interpretive Rule

The system should not assume a participant is one thing.

It should read identity movement as an ongoing relationship between core,
pattern, environment, and practice. The Identity Engine exists to keep that
relationship legible.
